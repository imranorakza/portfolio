02 — Framework

The account architecture I build on every engagement

This is the structure I take into every new account, then adapt to the business. Each layer exists to feed clean data and clear intent to the layer below it.

LAYER 01

Account

One account, one source of truth for billing, conversion actions, negative keyword lists and shared budgets. Everything below inherits from this layer.

LAYER 02

Campaigns

Few, consolidated campaigns organised by product line and goal — not by every possible segment. Consolidation is what lets Smart Bidding actually learn.

LAYER 03

Theme-Based Ad Groups

Groups of closely related keywords under one theme — one clear buying intent per group, enough volume for the data to mean something.

LAYER 04

Responsive Search Ads

A wide, genuinely different set of headlines and descriptions per ad group, with light, deliberate pinning — never a rebuilt static ad.

LAYER 05

Keywords

Start on Phrase Match for control, then let proven, profitable search terms graduate to Broad Match once Smart Bidding has data to work with.

LAYER 06

Dedicated Landing Pages

Every product line gets its own page — its own message, proof and form. No campaign ever points at a generic homepage.

LAYER 07

Conversion Tracking

Every form submission is tied back to the exact landing page, campaign and keyword that produced it — no guessing where leads came from.

LAYER 08

Optimization

A recurring loop across search terms, negatives, ad assets, landing pages and bids — not a one-time setup.

LAYER 09

Qualified Leads

The only metric that matters. Everything above this line exists to protect the quality of what lands here.

03 — Campaign Structure

Fewer, stronger campaigns outperform dozens of small ones

Google's own guidance and my own account audits agree on this: campaigns that are split too finely starve Smart Bidding of conversion data. Every campaign, budget change or bid strategy switch resets the learning phase, and split campaigns rarely collect enough conversions to leave it. Consolidation isn't a shortcut — it's what gives the algorithm enough signal to actually optimise.

I aim for campaigns that can each reach a steady 30+ conversions a month wherever the budget allows it. Below that, Smart Bidding is guessing. Above it, it's learning.

Separate campaigns when —

  • Brand vs Non-brandBranded traffic has near-guaranteed intent and a different bid logic — it shouldn't compete for budget with cold demand.
  • CountriesDifferent markets mean different budgets, languages and often different currencies and reporting needs.
  • LanguagesAd copy and landing pages can't be shared across languages, so the campaigns that hold them shouldn't be either.
  • Major product categoriesERP, HR & Payroll and CRM are different buyers with different budgets — they need separate reporting and separate scaling decisions.
  • Search vs Performance MaxDifferent inventory, different signals, different levers — mixing them in one campaign hides which one is doing the work.
  • Lead generation vs EcommerceCompletely different conversion actions and value models — they shouldn't share one bid strategy.

Don't separate campaigns for —

  • DevicesSmart Bidding already adjusts for device signals in real time — a separate campaign per device just fragments your data.
  • Keyword match typesSplitting Phrase and Broad into different campaigns was a control tactic from the manual-bidding era. It now just halves your conversion data.
  • Minor keyword variations"Cloud ERP software" and "cloud ERP system" belong in the same ad group, not their own campaigns.
  • Tiny budgetsA campaign that can't fund 30+ conversions a month will never leave the learning phase — merge it into something that can.
  • Too many campaigns generallyEvery extra campaign is one more place your own campaigns compete against each other in the same auction.

05 — Ad Groups

Theme-based ad groups, not SKAGs

Single Keyword Ad Groups made sense when bids were set manually, keyword by keyword. They don't anymore. Splitting an account into hundreds of one-keyword ad groups just spreads the same conversion data thinner — and Smart Bidding, and RSAs, both need volume to learn from. I build ad groups around one clear buying theme, with enough related keywords inside it that the ad group has real signal.

06 — Keyword Strategy

Start narrow, expand once the data proves it

I normally launch on Phrase Match. It gives me visibility into real search terms and keeps early spend under control while the account is still unproven. Once I have enough conversions and can see which search terms are actually profitable, I gradually expand into Broad Match, paired with Smart Bidding — because at that point Google has the conversion data it needs to find similar high-intent searches I'd never think to add manually.

Phrase Match
→
Prove profitable search terms
→
Broad Match + Smart Bidding

This sequence balances control early on with scalability later — I'm not handing the algorithm a blank cheque before it has earned the trust.

07 — Bidding Strategy

Let the account earn its way into automation

I start every new campaign on Maximize Conversions and let Google collect real conversion data without forcing a target on it too early. Once a campaign has built up roughly 30+ quality conversions and performance has stabilised, I'll consider moving it to Target CPA — but only if the business goals actually call for tighter cost control at that stage. There's no universal rule here. Every account, every budget and every sales cycle is different, and I make that call based on what the data shows, not a template.

08 — Ad Copy

Responsive Search Ads, built to actually get tested

Google gives you up to 15 headlines and 4 descriptions per RSA, and the biggest mistake I still see is advertisers writing 15 versions of the same sentence. Every headline needs to stand on its own — Google may only show two or three at a time.

ASSETS

Use the full asset set

15 unique headlines and all 4 descriptions, each highlighting a genuinely different angle — keyword, benefit, proof, offer, differentiator.

PINNING

Pin sparingly, never solo

When I do pin, I pin 2–3 unique variants to a position, never one — a single pinned line just rebuilds a static ad and stops the testing.

AD STRENGTH

A floor, not a target

I aim for "Good" or "Excellent" as a completeness check, then judge the ad on CTR, conversion rate and cost per lead — not the score itself.

What I actually do: I include at least one clear CTA headline and one CTA description in every RSA, and leave the rest of the asset mix flexible so Google's machine learning has room to optimise the combinations around it.

11 — SEO + Google Ads

Paid search and SEO feed each other, not compete

I treat Google Ads and SEO as one system rather than two departments. Google Ads tells you, in weeks not months, exactly which messages get clicks and conversions. SEO takes that proof and builds long-term, unpaid visibility around it — and what SEO learns about search behaviour feeds straight back into the next round of ad copy and keyword targeting.

STEP 1

Ads prove the message

Winning Google Ads headlines become website content once they've proven themselves against real buyers.

STEP 2

Landing pages support SEO

Dedicated, well-structured product pages built for Google Ads also became strong organic pages in their own right.

STEP 3

SEO improves Ads

Organic search term and ranking data feeds new keyword and messaging ideas back into the paid account.

Using this loop, I helped achieve page-1 rankings for our most important ERP software keywords, while the ad account kept improving in parallel.
